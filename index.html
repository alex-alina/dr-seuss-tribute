Here's a short biography of Dr. Seuss's life:

Theodor Seuss Geisel was an American author best known for authoring more than 60 children's books under the pen name Doctor Seuss (abbreviated Dr. Seuss). His work includes several of the most popular children's books of all time.

Geisel was born and raised in Springfield, Massachusetts and attended Dartmouth College, graduating in 1925. After graduating from Dartmouth, he entered Lincoln College, Oxford, intending to earn a PhD in English literature.

He left Oxford in 1927, without earning a degree, to begin his career as an illustrator and cartoonist for Vanity Fair, Life, and various other publications. He also worked as an illustrator for advertising campaigns, most notably for FLIT and Standard Oil, and as a political cartoonist for the New York newspaper.

Dr. Seuss published his first children's book And to Think That I Saw It on Mulberry Street in 1937, but as World War II began, he turned to political cartoons, drawing over 400 in two years as editorial cartoonist for the left-leaning New York City daily newspaper.

After the war, Geisel and his wife moved to La Jolla, California, where he returned to writing children's books. There he wrote many of his most appreciated books.

Geisel died of oral cancer on September 24, 1991 at his home in La Jolla at the age of 87.

Some of his most appreciated publications are:

  • 1955 - If I Ran the Zoo
  • 1955 - Horton Hears a Who!
  • 1956 - If I Ran the Circus
  • 1957 - The Cat in the Hat
  • 1957 - How the Grinch Stole Christmas!
  • 1960 - Green Eggs and Ham
